## Positioning Statement

Strata is the tool that makes your strategy self-navigating — built for executives who can't be in the room when the document lands.

---

## Three Tone Words

**Exacting. Credible. Unsentimental.**

- **Exacting** — We name numbers. We name competitors. We name the specific problem. "$4.2M, not 'meaningful ARR growth.'" Nothing is approximate when precision is available.
- **Credible** — We earn authority by acknowledging trade-offs and imperfections, not by claiming we're the best at everything. The demo's "win rate tracking 6 points below US — within expected range" is Strata's voice at its best. We say what's actually true.
- **Unsentimental** — We don't inspire. We solve. There's no "we believe in a world where..." There's "your only options were slides or a full day of custom work. This is the third option." The value is concrete, the language is spare, and the emotion comes from clarity, not warmth.

---

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Specificity is the argument
When you can say a number, say it. "$3,200 of senior executive time on a single document." "30 minutes, not a full day." "8 section types." Generic claims — "saves you time," "powerful features," "easy to use" — communicate nothing. Specificity communicates competence.

### 2. The document speaks for itself when the author is gone
This is the core product insight and it should show in the copy. Strata doesn't sell convenience — it sells the artifact. "Self-navigating," "self-convincing," "consumed asynchronously." The audience isn't in the room. That's the whole point. Copy that leans on "you'll love how fast this is" misses it. Copy that says "a CEO who won't sit through 60 slides can still navigate this alone" gets it.

### 3. Name the alternative, then reject it
The problem section on the landing page does this perfectly: "your only options are compressing it into slides or spending a day building something custom." Never float the benefit in a vacuum. Show what the user was doing before and why that failed. Then offer the third option.

### 4. Strategic imperfections build trust
In boardroom copy — demo content, case-study language, and anywhere we speak in a user's voice — acknowledging a flaw is more persuasive than hiding it. "Win rate 22% vs. 28% US benchmark — expected variance for new market" is more credible than "win rate on track." Strata documents are trustworthy because they don't perform. Our copy should do the same.

### 5. Short sentences land harder
The hero headline is seven words. "Strategy that speaks for itself." The problem statement is two sentences. If a sentence runs past 25 words, split it. If a paragraph runs past 4 lines, cut it. Density is not depth. Specificity is.

### 6. We are not impressed by our own product
Copy never marvels at how cool Strata is. No exclamation points. No "incredible," "amazing," or "stunning." If the product is impressive, the description of what it does will convey that. "A shareable URL. Self-navigating, interactive, and polished. No login to view." Period. Done.

### 7. One idea per sentence, one job per section
Every heading answers one question. Every paragraph advances one argument. Don't load multiple claims into a single sentence. "Paste your content, let AI structure it, and share something interactive, polished, and alive — in minutes" works because "polished and alive" is one idea (the artifact is real), and "in minutes" is a separate claim that contrasts with the implied "a full day."

## Forbidden Phrases

**Never use these.** Not in marketing copy, not in UI, not in demo content, not in email.

**AI-slop markers:**
- unlock / unlocks / unlocking
- unleash
- seamlessly / seamless
- delve / dive deep / dive into
- leverage (as a verb — "leverage AI," "leverage insights")
- journey (as a metaphor — "your strategy journey")
- empower / empowers / empowering
- revolutionize / revolutionary
- game-changer / game-changing
- cutting-edge
- best-in-class
- next-level
- transform your [anything]
- transformative
- reimagining / redefining
- "drive [noun] insights"
- at scale (except in precise technical context)
- turnkey
- holistic
- robust (except in technical accuracy context — "robust data model" is fine; "robust solution" is not)
- world-class
- supercharge
- harness
- innovative / innovation (unless quoting someone else)
- streamline / streamlined

**Structural clichés:**
- "We believe..." (manifesto-style openers)
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "More than just a [tool]..."
- "The future of [category]"
- "Built for the way you work"
- Any sentence that begins with "Whether you're..."

**Soft hedging:**
- "Meaningful [metric]" — say the number or don't say it
- "Significant improvements" — quantify or cut
- "Some of the most" — say the most or don't say "most"

## Voice by Surface

### Landing Page
**Character:** Confident, blunt, structured. Short sentences. Bold claims earned by specificity.

Rules:
- Hero headline: 5-10 words maximum. State the core value, nothing else.
- Problem section: name the two alternatives that fail, then offer the third option.
- How-it-works: action verbs, present tense. "Drop your content." "AI structures it." "Share a link."
- Section headers: declarative, not clever. "How it works." "8 interactive building blocks." Not "The Magic Happens Here."
- CTAs: concrete and motion-forward. "Start creating" and "See it in action" beat "Learn more" and "Get started."

From the live page — keep this energy:
> "Turn strategic content into something interactive, shareable, and alive — in minutes, not days. No slides. No coding. Just a link."

### Demo Content
**Character:** Boardroom-ready. Data-dense. Speaks in a user's voice, not Strata's.

Rules:
- Name companies, people, numbers. "Sarah Chen — ex-Gong EMEA. Built their UK team from 0 to £8M ARR in 14 months."
- Include strategic imperfections. "Win rate: 22% vs. 28% US benchmark — expected variance for new market." Not every number should be green.
- Use real metrics from real categories. Not "strong pipeline performance" — "$1.8M qualified pipeline with 60% in active evaluation."
- Callouts add perspective, not hype. "The market is open." "Pipeline 15% ahead of plan. Closed ARR 8% behind." Both. Always both.
- Deprioritized segments belong in the document. "Southern Europe — Deprioritized" is more trustworthy than pretending there's no trade-off.

### Editor UI Microcopy
**Character:** Terse. Action-oriented. Never explains what the user can see with their own eyes.

Rules:
- Buttons: verb-first. "Rewrite section." "Reorder." "Add section." Not "Section Reordering Tool."
- Tooltips: one sentence, no period. "Drag to reorder sections" not "You can reorder your sections by dragging them."
- Labels: noun only when unambiguous. "Title," "Summary," "Detail." Never "Your Section Title."
- Never use "Please" — it adds friction without warmth.
- Never describe the action the user just completed ("Section saved!") unless the save was ambiguous or slow.

### Empty States
**Character:** Warm but instructive. Points forward. Never performs cleverness.

Rules:
- One concrete action, not an invitation to imagine. "Drop a file or paste your content to get started." Not "Your canvas awaits."
- No humor. No puns. Empty states are task states, not personality moments.
- If the state is recoverable, say how. "No documents yet — create your first one." Not "Nothing here yet!"
- If the state has a reason (trial limit, plan restriction), say the reason plainly: "You've reached your 2-document limit on the free plan. Upgrade to continue." No softening.

### Error Messages
**Character:** Human, specific, and solution-forward. Never blame the user.

Rules:
- Name what failed: "We couldn't save your document" not "Something went wrong."
- Offer the next step: "Try again" or "Check your connection and retry" — always one concrete action.
- If an error is recoverable, say so. "Your draft is saved locally — we'll sync when your connection returns."
- If it isn't recoverable, be honest. Don't say "Try again in a few minutes" if it won't matter.
- Never use "Oops" — it's coy about a real failure.

### Email / Waitlist Communication
**Character:** First-person singular. Jon's voice, not Strata's corporate voice. Personal, direct, and honest about where the product is.

Rules:
- Open with context, not a subject line summary. "I'm building Strata because I spent a full day in Claude Code to produce a single strategy document — and that felt broken."
- Acknowledge what doesn't exist yet. Early users signed up for something unfinished. Respect that.
- No "we" unless there's actually a team. Use "I" until it's not accurate.
- Waitlist emails should feel like a founder's note, not a product newsletter. Length: 150-300 words max.

## Length Rules

| Surface | Headline | Body paragraph | CTA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Landing hero | 5-10 words | 1-2 sentences | 2-3 words |
| Landing section | 4-8 words | 2-4 sentences | 2-4 words |
| Demo section title | 2-5 words | N/A (data speaks) | N/A |
| Demo callout | 1-2 sentences | — | — |
| UI button | 1-3 words | — | — |
| UI tooltip | 1 sentence, no period | — | — |
| Empty state | 1 sentence | 1 follow-on sentence max | 2-3 words |
| Error message | 1 sentence naming the failure | 1 sentence with next step | 2-4 words |
| Email subject | 5-9 words | 150-300 words total | 3-5 words |

**Sentence length:** Target 10-18 words. Hard cap at 30. If you're over 30, split the sentence — you're carrying two ideas.

**Paragraph length:** 2-4 sentences for marketing copy. 1-3 for UI. Single-sentence paragraphs are fine and often better.

## Brands to Emulate

**Linear (product copy)**
Why: Precise, minimal, confident without being arrogant. Says exactly what the product does. "Built for speed." "Every millisecond matters." They don't oversell — they describe and trust the product to land. Their changelog reads like product journalism, not a feature list.

**Basecamp (plainspoken honesty)**
Why: They've never been afraid to say what they won't do. "We don't do meetings" is copy. Their pricing pages explain trade-offs in plain English. They sound like a person wrote it, not a committee approved it.

**Stripe (technical precision)**
Why: They write to people who need to trust them with money. Every claim is specific. Every explanation is accurate. The precision itself is the credibility signal. Not "easy to integrate" — "seven lines of code."

---

## Brands to Avoid Sounding Like

**Gamma (hype-driven)**
Problem: "Breathtaking AI-generated decks." "Stunning presentations in seconds." Superlatives aren't arguments. Their homepage reads like a demo reel with no evidence. When everything is stunning, nothing is.

**Generic SaaS landing pages**
Problem: "Empower your team." "Unlock insights." "Seamlessly integrate with your favorite tools." These are filler phrases that say nothing and make every product sound identical. They are the copy equivalent of stock photography.

**We-believe manifestos**
Problem: "We believe the best strategies deserve to be heard." "We believe every team deserves tools that work as hard as they do." These are values dressed as vision — they signal nothing differentiated. Strata's belief system shows in the product, not the homepage.

## Guardrails for the Copy Agent

These rules are not suggestions. They are boundaries.

**Tier 1 — Auto-merge after review window (48 hours):**
- Editor UI microcopy (button labels, tooltips, form labels)
- Empty states
- Error messages
- In-product notifications

**Tier 2 — Jon's explicit approval required before merge:**
- Landing page (`src/app/page.tsx`) — never touch marketing copy without approval
- Pricing page (`src/app/pricing/`) — never touch, ever, under any circumstances
- Any copy that names a competitor
- Any copy that includes a number or metric (pricing, feature counts, time claims)
- Demo content in `src/app/demo/page.tsx`
- Email copy or waitlist communications

**Behavioral rules:**
1. Never introduce a forbidden phrase, even as a "cleaner" rewrite. If the current copy says "unlock," flag it — don't replace it with "seamlessly access."
2. When a rewrite changes the meaning of a claim (even slightly), show both versions in the PR description and let Jon pick.
3. When in doubt about tone, default to shorter, more specific, and less clever. Strata copy errors toward plainness, not personality.
4. Never add exclamation points. If the sentence needs one to land, the sentence is the problem.
5. Surface area rule: a PR should touch one surface (either landing page or UI or email — not all three). Mixed-surface PRs hide scope.
6. Do not rewrite copy that is working. "If it's specific, numbered, and short — leave it alone" is the operating principle.
